# Pre-Program Reading List — CS Education
## Grounded in *How People Learn II* (National Academies, 2018)

**Professor Maine | Evangel University**  
**Goal:** General scholarly grounding before Fall 2026 doctoral program entry  
**Focus:** CS Education — pedagogy, equity, cognition, and cybersecurity curriculum

## How to Use This List

This reading list is organized around the seven major themes of *How People Learn II* (HPL2), mapped onto CS education scholarship. Each theme connects the cognitive and learning science of HPL2 to what that science means specifically for teaching and learning computer science at the undergraduate level.

**Reading Tiers (use with the [Adler Worksheet](../docs/adler-reading-worksheet.md)):**

| Tier | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ⭐⭐⭐ | Essential | Read analytically (full Adler worksheet) before Fall 2026 |
| ⭐⭐ | Important | Read analytically — can extend into Year 1 coursework |
| ⭐ | Recommended | Inspectional read now; return analytically as relevant |

**Suggested sequence:** Read the anchor text (HPL2) first. Then move theme by theme, pairing one book with 2–3 articles before moving on. Log all sessions in your [weekly study log](../independent-study/).

## Theme 1 — Prior Knowledge & Misconceptions in CS
*HPL2 connection: New knowledge is built on what learners already know — and blocked by what they think they know.*

In CS education, this means students arrive with deeply held mental models about how computers work, what programming is, and who "belongs" in computing. Instructors who ignore these models produce persistent misconceptions.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | *Learner-Centered Design of Computing Education: Research on Computing for Everyone* | Mark Guzdial | 2015 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 1.2 | *Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning* | Brown, Roediger & McDaniel | 2014 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 1.3 | *How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School* (Expanded Ed.) | Bransford, Brown & Cocking (Eds.) | 2000 | ⭐⭐ |

**1.1 — Guzdial (2015):** The most important single book in this list. Guzdial synthesizes decades of CS education research and argues for a learner-centered approach grounded in the cognitive science of how novices learn to program. Read this analytically and take detailed notes — it will scaffold your entire literature review.

**1.2 — Brown et al. (2014):** Accessible summary of cognitive psychology research on learning and memory. Covers retrieval practice, interleaving, and desirable difficulties — all of which have direct applications to CS pedagogy. A bridge between HPL2 and classroom practice.

**1.3 — Bransford et al. (2000):** The original HPL. Though HPL2 updates it, this remains essential because it established the framework your field still uses. Read chapters on prior knowledge and transfer.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 1.4 | McCracken, M., et al. (2001). A multi-national, multi-institutional study of assessment of programming skills of first-year CS students. *ACM SIGCSE Bulletin, 33*(4), 125–180. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.5 | Lister, R., et al. (2004). A multi-national study of reading and tracing skills in novice programmers. *ACM SIGCSE Bulletin, 36*(4), 119–150. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.6 | Guzdial, M. (2003). A media computation course for non-majors. *Proceedings of ACM SIGCSE*, 104–108. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.7 | Dehnadi, S., & Bornat, R. (2006). The camel has two humps. *Middlesex University Technical Report*. | Freely available online |

**1.4 — McCracken et al. (2001):** Landmark multi-institutional study revealing that most first-year CS students struggle with basic programming tasks despite completing intro courses. Foundational for understanding what prior "knowledge" students actually carry.

**1.5 — Lister et al. (2004):** Demonstrates that novice programmers need to learn to *read* code before they can write it — directly challenges typical CS1 sequencing. Essential for rethinking intro curriculum.

**1.6 — Guzdial (2003):** Introduces Media Computation as a context-first approach to CS1. Shows how using meaningful, culturally relevant contexts changes what prior knowledge students activate.

**1.7 — Dehnadi & Bornat (2006):** The controversial "camel" paper — argues that consistent mental models at the start of CS1 predict success. Provocative and debated; read it to understand the prior knowledge debate in your field.

## Theme 2 — Motivation, Identity & Belonging in CS
*HPL2 connection: Learners' beliefs about themselves — whether they "belong" and whether they can succeed — directly shape what they learn and whether they persist.*

This is perhaps the most critical theme for CS education, where identity-based barriers drive attrition, especially among women and underrepresented groups.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | *Unlocking the Clubhouse: Women in Computing* | Margolis & Fisher | 2002 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 2.2 | *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* | Carol S. Dweck | 2006 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 2.3 | *Stuck in the Shallow End: Education, Race, and Computing* | Margolis, Estrella, Goode, Holme & Nao | 2008 | ⭐⭐⭐ |

**2.1 — Margolis & Fisher (2002):** Foundational ethnographic study of why women leave CS at Carnegie Mellon. Identifies the "clubhouse" culture of CS departments as a structural barrier, not a talent gap. Essential reading.

**2.2 — Dweck (2006):** Research on fixed vs. growth mindset with direct application to CS education. Students who believe programming ability is fixed are more likely to drop out after early struggle. Widely cited in CS education reform efforts.

**2.3 — Margolis et al. (2008):** Extends the Unlocking the Clubhouse analysis to K–12, particularly urban schools. Argues that computing education equity is a systemic issue, not an individual one. Highly relevant to your undergraduate teaching context.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 2.4 | Lewis, C. M., Anderson, R., & Yasuhara, K. (2016). I don't code all day: Fitting in computer science when the stereotypes don't fit. *Proceedings of ICER*, 23–32. | ACM Digital Library |
| 2.5 | Cheryan, S., Plaut, V. C., Davies, P. G., & Steele, C. M. (2009). Ambient belonging: How stereotypical cues impact gender participation in computer science. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 97*(6), 1045–1060. | APA PsycNet |
| 2.6 | Beyer, S. (2014). Why are women underrepresented in computer science? Gender differences in stereotypes, self-efficacy, values, and interests. *Journal of Educational Computing Research, 51*(3), 306–317. | SAGE Journals |
| 2.7 | Blaney, J. M., & Stout, J. G. (2017). Examining the relationship between introductory computing course experiences, self-efficacy, and belonging for women in CS. *Proceedings of SIGCSE*, 69–74. | ACM Digital Library |

**2.4 — Lewis et al. (2016):** Qualitative study of how students who don't match the "computer science stereotype" navigate identity in CS programs. Directly relevant to your students at a faith-based institution.

**2.5 — Cheryan et al. (2009):** Experimental study showing that even environmental cues (Star Trek posters, soda cans in a lab) affect women's sense of belonging in CS. Striking, concise, and widely cited.

**2.6 — Beyer (2014):** Synthesizes research on gender gaps in CS through the lens of self-efficacy and interest — two variables you can directly address through pedagogy.

**2.7 — Blaney & Stout (2017):** Examines how the CS1 experience specifically shapes belonging and self-efficacy for women. Connects to your intro course design.

## Theme 3 — Culture, Context & Equity in CS Education
*HPL2 connection: Learning is always culturally situated. Context isn't background noise — it IS the learning environment.*

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3.1 | *Culturally Responsive Teaching: Theory, Research, and Practice* (3rd ed.) | Geneva Gay | 2018 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 3.2 | *CS for All: Integrating Computational Thinking into K–12 Science* | Guzdial & Ericson | 2019 | ⭐⭐ |

**3.1 — Gay (2018):** The definitive text on culturally responsive pedagogy. Not written for CS specifically, but its framework has been widely adapted into CS education research. Essential for grounding equity work theoretically.

**3.2 — Guzdial & Ericson (2019):** Applies culturally responsive and learner-centered principles to broadening participation in computing. Bridges theory and curriculum design.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 3.3 | Scott, K. A., Sheridan, K. M., & Clark, K. (2015). Culturally responsive computing: A theory revisited. *Learning, Media and Technology, 40*(4), 412–436. | Taylor & Francis |
| 3.4 | Goode, J. (2008). Connecting K–16 culture and computing: Sisters in science. *Proceedings of ICER*, 35–46. | ACM Digital Library |
| 3.5 | Washington, A. N. (2020). When twice as good isn't enough: The case for culturally responsive computing education. *Proceedings of SIGCSE*, 314–320. | ACM Digital Library |
| 3.6 | Barr, V., & Stephenson, C. (2011). Bringing computational thinking to K–12: What is involved and what is the role of the computer science education community? *ACM Inroads, 2*(1), 48–54. | ACM Digital Library |

**3.3 — Scott et al. (2015):** Revisits and updates the framework of culturally responsive computing. Useful theoretical grounding for how to design CS curricula that acknowledge cultural context.

**3.4 — Goode (2008):** Case study connecting school culture to computing participation — excellent example of qualitative research methodology in CS education.

**3.5 — Washington (2020):** A direct challenge to "twice as good" frameworks in CS equity — argues that individual excellence isn't enough when systemic barriers exist. Provocative and current.

**3.6 — Barr & Stephenson (2011):** Landmark article on computational thinking in K–12 — foundational for understanding how CS education reform has been framed at the policy level.

## Theme 4 — Metacognition & Self-Regulation in CS
*HPL2 connection: Students who can monitor and regulate their own learning outperform those who cannot. Teaching metacognition is teaching.*

In CS, this means students need to know *how* to debug, *how* to read error messages, and *how* to know when they understand versus when they've memorized.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4.1 | *Creating Significant Learning Experiences: An Integrated Approach to Designing College Courses* | L. Dee Fink | 2003 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 4.2 | *What the Best College Teachers Do* | Ken Bain | 2004 | ⭐⭐ |

**4.1 — Fink (2003):** Provides a framework for designing courses that create lasting learning, including how to build metacognitive awareness into course structure. Widely used in college teaching development.

**4.2 — Bain (2004):** Qualitative study of what exceptional college professors do differently. Short, readable, and grounded in research on how students learn in higher ed.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 4.3 | Loksa, D., Ko, A. J., Jernigan, W., Oleson, A., Mendez, C. J., & Burnett, M. M. (2016). The role of programming environment design in novice programming self-efficacy. *ACM Transactions on Computing Education, 17*(3), 1–31. | ACM Digital Library |
| 4.4 | Prather, J., Pettit, R., Becker, B. A., Denny, P., Loksa, D., & Albrecht, A. (2019). First things first: Providing metacognitive scaffolding for interpreting problem prompts. *Proceedings of SIGCSE*, 531–537. | ACM Digital Library |
| 4.5 | Whalley, J., et al. (2006). An Australasian study of reading and comprehension skills in novice programmers. *Proceedings of the 8th Australasian Computing Education Conference*, 243–252. | ACM Digital Library |

**4.3 — Loksa et al. (2016):** Examines how the design of programming tools shapes students' self-efficacy and metacognitive behavior. Links environment design to HPL2's emphasis on context.

**4.4 — Prather et al. (2019):** Demonstrates that teaching students *how to read a programming problem* — before attempting to solve it — significantly improves outcomes. Direct classroom application.

**4.5 — Whalley et al. (2006):** Multi-institutional study of novice CS students' reading and tracing skills. Foundational for understanding self-regulation gaps in intro programming courses.

## Theme 5 — Transfer & Deep Learning in CS
*HPL2 connection: The goal of education is transfer — the ability to apply learning in new contexts. Surface learning produces performance; deep learning produces transfer.*

In CS education, transfer is the difference between students who can only solve problems they've seen before versus those who can tackle novel problems.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5.1 | *Understanding by Design* (2nd ed.) | Wiggins & McTighe | 2005 | ⭐⭐ |

**5.1 — Wiggins & McTighe (2005):** Introduces "backward design" — starting from the desired transfer before designing instruction. Foundational for curriculum development in any field, including CS.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 5.2 | Perkins, D. N., & Salomon, G. (1992). Transfer of learning. *International Encyclopedia of Education* (2nd ed.). | Freely available online |
| 5.3 | Soloway, E., & Spohrer, J. C. (Eds.). (1989). Studying the novice programmer. *Lawrence Erlbaum Associates*. [Select chapters] | University library |
| 5.4 | Guzdial, M., & Forte, A. (2005). Design process for a non-majors computing course. *Proceedings of SIGCSE*, 207–211. | ACM Digital Library |

**5.2 — Perkins & Salomon (1992):** The definitive theoretical treatment of transfer in education. Short, highly cited, and essential for understanding what deep learning means in your field.

**5.3 — Soloway & Spohrer (1989):** Classic collection on novice programmer cognition. Though older, it remains the foundation for understanding how CS transfer fails and why.

**5.4 — Guzdial & Forte (2005):** Applies transfer-oriented design to a non-majors CS course. Shows how context and relevance improve transfer outcomes.

## Theme 6 — Technology as a Learning Environment in CS
*HPL2 connection: Technology changes not just how we teach, but how students think and what counts as knowledge.*

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 6.1 | Ericson, B., & Morrison, B. (2020). Evaluating the efficiency and effectiveness of adaptive parsons problems. *Proceedings of SIGCSE*, 883–889. | ACM Digital Library |
| 6.2 | Denny, P., Luxton-Reilly, A., & Simon, B. (2008). Evaluating a new exam question: Parsons problems. *Proceedings of ICER*, 113–124. | ACM Digital Library |
| 6.3 | Becker, B. A., et al. (2019). Compiler error messages considered unhelpful: The landscape of text-based programming error messages. *Proceedings of WiPSCE*. | ACM Digital Library |
| 6.4 | Porter, L., Guzdial, M., McDowell, C., & Simon, B. (2013). Success in introductory programming: What works? *Communications of the ACM, 56*(8), 34–36. | ACM Digital Library |

**6.1–6.2 — Ericson & Morrison / Denny et al.:** Parsons problems (drag-and-drop code blocks) represent a technology-mediated approach to reducing cognitive load in novice programming. Highly relevant to your teaching.

**6.3 — Becker et al. (2019):** Reviews how compiler error messages fail novice learners — a technology design problem with significant pedagogical consequences.

**6.4 — Porter et al. (2013):** Synthesizes research on what actually works in CS1 — peer instruction, pair programming, media computation. Essential summary article.

## ICER Spotlight — High-Priority Empirical Research

The **ACM International Computing Education Research (ICER)** conference is where the most rigorous, peer-reviewed empirical work in CS education is published. Now that you have SIGCSE/ACM access, browsing ICER proceedings by theme is one of the highest-value pre-program activities you can do.

> **How to access:** Go to [dl.acm.org](https://dl.acm.org) → search "ICER" → filter by year or keyword. All proceedings from 2003 onward are available with your membership.

### Recommended ICER Papers by Theme

#### Novice Programming & Cognition

| # | Citation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| I.1 | Sorva, J. (2012). Visual program simulation in introductory programming education. *Doctoral dissertation, Aalto University* (widely cited in ICER). | Definitive treatment of how notional machines — mental models of how code executes — form in novices. Essential for understanding CS1 cognition. |
| I.2 | Tew, A. E., & Guzdial, M. (2011). The FCS1: A language independent assessment of CS1 knowledge. *Proceedings of ICER*, 111–120. | Introduces the only validated, language-independent assessment of CS1 learning outcomes. Foundational for any research on intro CS effectiveness. |
| I.3 | Lister, R. (2011). Concrete and other neo-Piagetian forms of reasoning in the novice programmer. *Proceedings of ACSC*, 9–18. | Applies Piagetian developmental theory to CS1 — argues novices need concrete operational thinking before abstract programming tasks. Changes how you sequence instruction. |

| I.5 | Alvarado, C., Dodds, Z., & Libeskind-Hadas, R. (2012). Increasing women's participation in computing at Harvey Mudd College. *ACM Inroads, 3*(4), 55–64. | Case study of a successful, replicable departmental intervention that significantly increased women's enrollment in CS. ### How to Build Your Own ICER Reading Queue

Use these search strategies in the ACM Digital Library to extend beyond this curated list:

```
1. Go to dl.acm.org
2. Search: "ICER" in proceedings title
3. Filter by keyword:
   - "introductory programming" → novice cognition literature
   - "broadening participation" → equity and identity literature
   - "self-efficacy" → motivation literature
   - "cybersecurity education" → your specialization
   - "active learning" → pedagogy literature
4. Sort by "Citations" to surface most-cited (most influential) work
5. Save PDFs to Google Drive; add URLs to Instapaper for annotation
```

**Set a Google Scholar alert** for: `"computing education" OR "CS education" site:dl.acm.org` to receive new ICER and SIGCSE papers as they publish.

## Notes & Access

- **SIGCSE Membership (joined May 2026):** Full access to ACM Digital Library through your SIGCSE membership — all SIGCSE Symposium and ICER proceedings are available.
