## Theme 1 — Prior Knowledge & Misconceptions in CS
*HPL2 connection: New knowledge is built on what learners already know — and blocked by what they think they know.*

In CS education, this means students arrive with deeply held mental models about how computers work, what programming is, and who "belongs" in computing. Instructors who ignore these models produce persistent misconceptions.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | *Learner-Centered Design of Computing Education: Research on Computing for Everyone* | Mark Guzdial | 2015 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 1.2 | *Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning* | Brown, Roediger & McDaniel | 2014 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 1.3 | *How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School* (Expanded Ed.) | Bransford, Brown & Cocking (Eds.) | 2000 | ⭐⭐ |

**1.1 — Guzdial (2015):** The most important single book in this list. Guzdial synthesizes decades of CS education research and argues for a learner-centered approach grounded in the cognitive science of how novices learn to program. Read this analytically and take detailed notes — it will scaffold your entire literature review.

**1.2 — Brown et al. (2014):** Accessible summary of cognitive psychology research on learning and memory. Covers retrieval practice, interleaving, and desirable difficulties — all of which have direct applications to CS pedagogy. A bridge between HPL2 and classroom practice.

**1.3 — Bransford et al. (2000):** The original HPL. Though HPL2 updates it, this remains essential because it established the framework your field still uses. Read chapters on prior knowledge and transfer.

### Journal Articles

| # | Citation | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| 1.4 | McCracken, M., et al. (2001). A multi-national, multi-institutional study of assessment of programming skills of first-year CS students. *ACM SIGCSE Bulletin, 33*(4), 125–180. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.5 | Lister, R., et al. (2004). A multi-national study of reading and tracing skills in novice programmers. *ACM SIGCSE Bulletin, 36*(4), 119–150. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.6 | Guzdial, M. (2003). A media computation course for non-majors. *Proceedings of ACM SIGCSE*, 104–108. | ACM Digital Library |
| 1.7 | Dehnadi, S., & Bornat, R. (2006). The camel has two humps. *Middlesex University Technical Report*. | Freely available online |

**1.4 — McCracken et al. (2001):** Landmark multi-institutional study revealing that most first-year CS students struggle with basic programming tasks despite completing intro courses. Foundational for understanding what prior "knowledge" students actually carry.

**1.5 — Lister et al. (2004):** Demonstrates that novice programmers need to learn to *read* code before they can write it — directly challenges typical CS1 sequencing. Essential for rethinking intro curriculum.

**1.6 — Guzdial (2003):** Introduces Media Computation as a context-first approach to CS1. Shows how using meaningful, culturally relevant contexts changes what prior knowledge students activate.

**1.7 — Dehnadi & Bornat (2006):** The controversial "camel" paper — argues that consistent mental models at the start of CS1 predict success. Provocative and debated; read it to understand the prior knowledge debate in your field.

## Theme 2 — Motivation, Identity & Belonging in CS
*HPL2 connection: Learners' beliefs about themselves — whether they "belong" and whether they can succeed — directly shape what they learn and whether they persist.*

This is perhaps the most critical theme for CS education, where identity-based barriers drive attrition, especially among women and underrepresented groups.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | *Unlocking the Clubhouse: Women in Computing* | Margolis & Fisher | 2002 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 2.2 | *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* | Carol S. Dweck | 2006 | ⭐⭐⭐ |
| 2.3 | *Stuck in the Shallow End: Education, Race, and Computing* | Margolis, Estrella, Goode, Holme & Nao | 2008 | ⭐⭐⭐ |

**2.1 — Margolis & Fisher (2002):** Foundational ethnographic study of why women leave CS at Carnegie Mellon. Identifies the "clubhouse" culture of CS departments as a structural barrier, not a talent gap. Essential reading.

**2.2 — Dweck (2006):** Research on fixed vs. growth mindset with direct application to CS education. Students who believe programming ability is fixed are more likely to drop out after early struggle. Widely cited in CS education reform efforts.

**2.3 — Margolis et al. (2008):** Extends the Unlocking the Clubhouse analysis to K–12, particularly urban schools. Argues that computing education equity is a systemic issue, not an individual one. Highly relevant to your undergraduate teaching context.

**2.4 — Lewis et al. (2016):** Qualitative study of how students who don't match the "computer science stereotype" navigate identity in CS programs. Directly relevant to your students at a faith-based institution.

**2.5 — Cheryan et al. (2009):** Experimental study showing that even environmental cues (Star Trek posters, soda cans in a lab) affect women's sense of belonging in CS. Striking, concise, and widely cited.

**2.6 — Beyer (2014):** Synthesizes research on gender gaps in CS through the lens of self-efficacy and interest — two variables you can directly address through pedagogy.

**2.7 — Blaney & Stout (2017):** Examines how the CS1 experience specifically shapes belonging and self-efficacy for women. Connects to your intro course design.

## Theme 4 — Metacognition & Self-Regulation in CS
*HPL2 connection: Students who can monitor and regulate their own learning outperform those who cannot. Teaching metacognition is teaching.*

In CS, this means students need to know *how* to debug, *how* to read error messages, and *how* to know when they understand versus when they've memorized.

**4.1 — Fink (2003):** Provides a framework for designing courses that create lasting learning, including how to build metacognitive awareness into course structure. Widely used in college teaching development.

**4.2 — Bain (2004):** Qualitative study of what exceptional college professors do differently. Short, readable, and grounded in research on how students learn in higher ed.

## Theme 5 — Transfer & Deep Learning in CS
*HPL2 connection: The goal of education is transfer — the ability to apply learning in new contexts. Surface learning produces performance; deep learning produces transfer.*

In CS education, transfer is the difference between students who can only solve problems they've seen before versus those who can tackle novel problems.

### Books

| # | Title | Author(s) | Year | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5.1 | *Understanding by Design* (2nd ed.) | Wiggins & McTighe | 2005 | ⭐⭐ |

**5.1 — Wiggins & McTighe (2005):** Introduces "backward design" — starting from the desired transfer before designing instruction. Foundational for curriculum development in any field, including CS.

### Journal Articles

**5.3 — Soloway & Spohrer (1989):** Classic collection on novice programmer cognition. Though older, it remains the foundation for understanding how CS transfer fails and why.

**5.4 — Guzdial & Forte (2005):** Applies transfer-oriented design to a non-majors CS course. Shows how context and relevance improve transfer outcomes.

## ICER Spotlight — High-Priority Empirical Research

The **ACM International Computing Education Research (ICER)** conference is where the most rigorous, peer-reviewed empirical work in CS education is published. Now that you have SIGCSE/ACM access, browsing ICER proceedings by theme is one of the highest-value pre-program activities you can do.

> **How to access:** Go to [dl.acm.org](https://dl.acm.org) → search "ICER" → filter by year or keyword. All proceedings from 2003 onward are available with your membership.
