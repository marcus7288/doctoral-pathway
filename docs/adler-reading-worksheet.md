## About This Worksheet

Mortimer Adler's *How to Read a Book* argues that most people read passively and therefore get little from what they read. True reading is an active, demanding conversation between reader and author. Adler identifies **four levels of reading** and provides rules for each. This worksheet guides you through all four levels for any scholarly text.

**Use this worksheet for:**
- Every book assigned in coursework
- Every foundational text in your literature review
- Any text you need to understand deeply, not just summarize

**Copy this file for each new text.** File as `reading-notes/[lastname-year-shorttitle].md`.

## Level 2 — Inspectional Reading
*"What is this book about, as a whole?"*

Inspectional reading is **systematic skimming** — done before reading the full text. It should take 15–45 minutes for most books. The goal is to understand the book's structure and argument before committing to a full reading.

### Stage 2A — Systematic Skimming (15–20 minutes)


**Step 5: Pivot Chapters**

Read the opening and closing pages of what look like the 2–3 most important chapters.


**Step 6: Final Pages**

Read the last 2–3 pages of the final chapter or epilogue.

```

What is the author's final argument or call to action?
```

### Stage 3C — Is It True? What of It? (Rules 9–15)

Adler insists: **do not criticize until you have fully understood.** Complete Stage 3A and 3B before answering these questions.

#### Rule 9 — Have You Completed Your Understanding? (Self-Check)

Before criticizing, confirm:
- [ ] I can state the book's main argument in one sentence (Rule 2)
- [ ] I have identified the key terms and know what the author means (Rule 5)
- [ ] I have located the main propositions (Rule 6)
- [ ] I have reconstructed the basic arguments (Rule 7)
- [ ] I know whether the author solved the stated problems (Rule 8)

If any box is unchecked, return to that rule before proceeding.

#### Rule 10 — Distinguish Between Knowledge and Personal Opinion

Before evaluating, be honest about your own position:

```
What did I believe about this topic BEFORE reading?


Has my view changed? If so, how?


Am I evaluating the author's argument on its merits, or reacting to disagreement with my prior view?

```

## Quick Reference — Adler's Four Levels

| Level | Name | Core Question | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Elementary | Can I understand the words? | Always — the baseline |
| **2** | Inspectional | What is this about as a whole? | Before committing to full read |
| **3** | Analytical | What does it say in detail? Is it true? | For all central texts |
| **4** | Syntopical | How does it fit with other texts? | When building literature review |

## Quick Reference — Adler's 15 Rules of Analytical Reading

**Stage A — What Is the Book About?**
1. Classify the book by kind and subject matter
2. State what the whole book is about in a single sentence
3. Enumerate the major parts in their order and outline them
4. Define the problem(s) the author is solving

**Stage B — What Is Being Said in Detail?**
5. Find the important words and come to terms with the author
6. Mark the most important sentences; find the propositions
7. Locate or construct the basic arguments in the book
8. Determine what the author's solutions are

**Stage C — Is It True? What of It?**
9. Do not criticize until you have completed your understanding
10. Do not disagree disputatiously — distinguish knowledge from opinion
11. Respect the difference between opinion and knowledge in your judgment
12. Show where the author is uninformed
13. Show where the author is misinformed
14. Show where the author's reasoning is illogical
15. Show where the author's analysis is incomplete
