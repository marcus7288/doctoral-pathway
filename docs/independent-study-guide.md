## Purpose of This Guide

Independent study in a doctoral program is not passive reading — it is active scholarly inquiry. This guide provides a structured framework for:

1. Planning and executing independent reading sessions
2. Producing evidence of scholarly engagement
3. Building toward comprehensive examinations and the dissertation
4. Maintaining a documentable, advisor-reviewable record of your intellectual growth

> **The core principle:** Every reading session should produce *something* — a note, a reflection, an annotation, a question. Undocumented study cannot be evaluated or built upon.

---

## Part 1 — Planning Your Independent Study

### 1.1 Setting Semester Goals

At the start of each term, define your independent study goals in writing and share them with your advisor. Use this structure:

```
Semester: [e.g., Summer 2027]
Primary Research Area: [topic]
Target Texts: [list 10–20 titles]
Credit Hours / Time Commitment: [hours per week]
Deliverable at Semester End: [e.g., annotated bibliography, literature review section, reading logs]
Connection to Dissertation: [how this reading advances your research]
```

Save this as `independent-study/[semester]/reading-list.md`.

### 1.2 Building a Reading List

A strong doctoral reading list is **intentional and tiered**:

| Tier | Type | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Foundational** | Seminal texts in your field | Establishes your scholarly grounding |
| **Tier 2 — Methodological** | Research methods & design texts | Prepares for dissertation methodology |
| **Tier 3 — Current** | Recent journal articles (last 5 years) | Shows awareness of current conversations |
| **Tier 4 — Adjacent** | Cross-disciplinary works | Broadens theoretical frameworks |

**Rule of thumb:** For every foundational book, read 3–5 recent journal articles in the same theme.

### 1.3 Weekly Time Budgeting

| Program Phase | Recommended Study Hours/Week |
|---|---|
| Year 1 (coursework active) | 5–8 hours independent study |
| Year 2 (coursework winding down) | 10–15 hours |
| Year 3 (ABD, dissertation) | 20–25 hours |

**Block scheduling tip:** Reserve 2–3 dedicated blocks per week (90 min each) for independent reading. Treat these as protected, non-negotiable appointments.

## Part 2 — During the Reading Session

### 2.1 The PQRST Reading Method

Use this structure for every scholarly text:

| Stage | Action |
|---|---|
| **P — Preview** | Read title, abstract, TOC, introduction, and conclusion first |
| **Q — Question** | Write 3–5 questions you want the text to answer before reading |
| **R — Read** | Read actively with pencil/stylus; annotate in Instapaper or physical copy |
| **S — Summarize** | After each major section, write a 2–3 sentence summary in your own words |
| **T — Test** | Review your questions — which were answered? What surprised you? |

### 2.2 Active Annotation Practices

Annotations are the primary evidence of engagement. Use a consistent system:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `★` or highlight | Key argument or main claim |
| `?` | Unclear, need to follow up |
| `!` | Surprising or counterintuitive |
| `→ diss` | Directly relevant to dissertation |
| `→ lit review` | Relevant to literature review |
| `cf.` | Compare with another source |
| `Q:` | A question this raises |
| `def:` | A definition to remember |

**In Instapaper:** Use the highlight + note feature. Export highlights at the end of each reading session and paste into your reading notes file.

### 2.3 Writing While Reading

Do not wait until you finish a text to start writing. Write:
- A 1–2 sentence response after every major section
- A running list of key terms encountered
- Any connection to other texts you've read
- Any questions you want to ask your advisor

## Part 3 — Producing Evidence of Work

### 3.1 Required Outputs for Each Text

For every book or long article you read, produce the following in your reading notes file:

```markdown
## [Author Last Name, Year] — [Short Title]

**Full Citation:** [APA or program style]
**Date Read:** [Start] – [End]
**Reading Method Used:** [Adler Analytical / PQRST / Inspectional]
**Assigned or Self-Selected:** [Assigned by advisor / Independent selection]

### 1. Pre-Reading (Inspectional)
[2–3 sentences: what did you expect this text to be about?]

### 2. Main Argument (in your own words)
[1 paragraph: what is the author's central claim?]

### 3. Key Terms & Concepts
- Term 1: definition
- Term 2: definition

### 4. Critical Summary by Section
[Brief notes on each major chapter or section]

### 5. Strongest Evidence / Best Examples Used
[What evidence did the author provide? Was it convincing?]

### 6. Connections to Other Sources
- Agrees with: [source]
- Challenges: [source]
- Extends: [source]

### 7. Critical Evaluation
[Do you agree? What are the limitations? What did the author miss?]

### 8. Relevance to Your Research
[How does this connect to your research questions / dissertation?]

### 9. Questions for Advisor or Further Investigation
- 
- 

### 10. Quotations Worth Keeping
> "[Quote]" (p. X)
```

### 3.2 Weekly Study Log

Maintain a `weekly-log.md` in each semester folder. Complete it every week:

```markdown
## Week of [Date]

**Total Study Hours This Week:** ___

### Sessions Completed
| Date | Text Read | Pages/Sections | Hours | Output Produced |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | |

### Key Insights This Week
[3–5 bullet points — what did you learn?]

### Questions/Confusions to Resolve
[What do you still not understand? What needs follow-up?]

### Next Week's Plan
[What will you read? What will you write?]

### Advisor Notes (if applicable)
[Any feedback from your advisor this week]
```

### 3.3 Monthly Reflection

At the end of each month, write a 300–500 word reflection:

- What themes are emerging across your reading?
- How has your understanding of your research area changed?
- What gaps in the literature are you noticing?
- What do you still need to read?
- How does this month's reading connect to your comprehensive exam prep or dissertation?

Save as `independent-study/[semester]/reflections/[month]-reflection.md`.

## Part 4 — Evidence Framework for Advisor Review

When meeting with your advisor, bring the following as evidence of independent study progress:

### 4.1 Meeting Packet (Prepare Before Each Advisor Meeting)

1. **Updated reading list** — highlight what has been completed since last meeting
2. **2–3 reading notes files** from texts completed since last meeting
3. **Current weekly log** showing study hours
4. **One-page synthesis** — how this reading connects to your emerging research questions
5. **Questions for the meeting** — written in advance

### 4.2 Semester-End Portfolio

At the end of each semester, compile a portfolio containing:

| Item | Format |
|---|---|
| Reading list with completion status | Markdown table |
| All reading notes files for the semester | Folder of `.md` files |
| Total study hours log | Summary table |
| Annotated bibliography additions | APA format entries |
| Semester reflection essay (500–800 words) | Markdown document |
| Next semester reading plan | Markdown document |

This portfolio serves as your official record of independent scholarly work and can be submitted to your advisor as a progress report.

## Part 5 — Best Practices for Doctoral Independent Study

### Do This

- **Read daily.** Even 20–30 minutes of focused reading compounds enormously over 3 years.
- **Write about everything you read.** If it's not written down, it didn't happen academically.
- **Read with your dissertation in mind.** Ask: *How does this fit into my literature review? Does this challenge my argument?*
- **Track everything in Git.** Commit your reading notes with descriptive messages. Your commit history is a timestamp-verified record of your scholarly work.
- **Use your tools consistently.** Instapaper for saving and annotating; @Voice for listening during commutes; Zotero for citations.
- **Discuss what you read.** Share insights with your advisor, peers, and even students. Teaching what you read deepens understanding.
- **Read across disciplines.** The best dissertations synthesize ideas from unexpected places.

### Avoid This

- Reading without annotating (passive consumption)
- Saving articles without reading them (Instapaper is not a filing cabinet)
- Going more than two weeks without reviewing your reading list with your advisor
- Writing notes so brief they're useless six months later
- Waiting until "the right time" to start — start now, even imperfectly

### The 3-Read Strategy (for Critical Texts)

For the most important works in your field, read the text three times:

1. **First read (Inspectional):** Skim — what is this book about? What is the argument?
2. **Second read (Analytical):** Read carefully — take full notes using the Adler worksheet
3. **Third read (Syntopical):** Re-read with your other sources in hand — how does this fit into the conversation?

## Part 6 — Using Git to Document Your Scholarly Work

Your GitHub repository is a timestamped, public-facing record of your doctoral work. Use it intentionally:

### Commit Message Conventions

```
Add reading notes: [Author Last] [Year] — [Short Title]
Update lit review: [theme or chapter]
Add weekly log: [Semester] Week [N]
Add monthly reflection: [Month Year]
Update reading list: [Semester]
Add annotated bibliography entry: [Author Last] [Year]
```

### Suggested Workflow

```bash
# After completing a reading session:
git add reading-notes/[filename].md
git add independent-study/[semester]/weekly-log.md
git commit -m "Add reading notes: Adler 1940 — How to Read a Book"
git push origin main
```

**Your commit history is your scholarly paper trail.** An advisor, committee member, or graduate school reviewer can see the date, time, and content of every piece of work you produced.

## Appendix A — Recommended Reading Workflow

```
1. Add text to Instapaper (from article, PDF, or web)
2. Listen to introductory sections via @Voice on commute
3. Complete Adler Inspectional Read (preview, skim)
4. Full reading session with Instapaper annotations
5. Export highlights → paste into reading notes .md file
6. Complete reading notes template (see Part 3.1)
7. Update weekly log with hours and output
8. Commit and push to GitHub
9. Add to annotated bibliography if using in lit review
10. Add citation to Zotero
```
